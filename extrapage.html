ODI CRICKET

A one Day International (ODI) is a type of limited-overs cricket game played between two international teams in which each team is given a certain amount of overs, currently 50, and the game can run up to 9 hours. This format is used for the Cricket World Cup, which is contested every four years. Limited Overs Internationals (LOI) refers to One Day International matches, although this word may also apply to Twenty20 International matches. They are big matches that represent the pinnacle of List A limited-overs cricket.


The international one-day game was developed in the late twentieth century. On January 5, 1971, the Melbourne Cricket Ground hosted the first One-Day International between Australia and England. When the third Test was washed out for the first three days, officials opted to call the game off and instead play a one-off one-day encounter with 40 eight-ball overs per side. By a margin of 5 wickets, Australia won the game. The ODIs were played using a red ball and white kits.


Kerry Packer founded the rival World Series Cricket competition in the late 1970s, and it introduced many of the features of One Day International cricket that are now standard, such as coloured uniforms, matches played at night under floodlights with a white ball and dark sight screens, and multiple camera angles, effects microphones to capture sounds from the players on the pitch, and on-screen graphics for television broadcasts. The WSC Australians in wattle gold versus the WSC West Indians in coral pink, contested at VFL Park in Melbourne on January 17, 1979, was the first match using colored outfits. Not only did this result in Packer's Channel 9 obtaining the television rights to cricket in Australia, but it also resulted in players all over the world being paid to play and becoming international pros, no longer requiring outside employment. Over time, matches using colored flannels and a white ball grew increasingly regular, and the use of white flannels and a red ball in ODIs was phased out in 2001.


The ICC ODI Rankings for teams, batsmen, bowlers, and all-rounders are maintained by the International Cricket Council, which is the governing body of international cricket. New Zealand is currently the best ODI team in the world.

T20 CRICKET

A normal Twenty20 match takes roughly three hours, with each innings running around 90 minutes and a 10-minute break in between. This is a considerably shorter version of the game than earlier versions, and it is more in line with other popular team sports. It was created to create a fast-paced game that would appeal to both spectators and television viewers.

TEST CRICKET

Test cricket is the longest format of cricket and is considered to be the highest level of the game. Test matches are contested between national representative teams that have been awarded Test status by the International Cricket Council, as determined and conferred (ICC). They're termed Tests because matches are mentally and physically demanding due to their duration and difficulty. A four-innings match between two teams of 11 players can last up to five days (or more in the past; contests with no time limit were known as Timeless Tests). It is widely regarded as the most comprehensive test of a team's endurance and ability.
The first legally recognized Test match was contested between England and Australia at the Melbourne Cricket Ground between March 15 and 19, 1877. (MCG). The International Cricket Council (ICC) revised the playing conditions for Test matches in October 2012, allowing for day/night Test matches. On the 27th and 1st of December 2015, Australia and New Zealand played the first day/night game at the Adelaide Oval in Adelaide.
